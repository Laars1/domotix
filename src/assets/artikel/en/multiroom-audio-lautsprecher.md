# Multiroom Audio: Why even non-audiophiles should consider built-in speakers and why Sonos is not always the best solution

Music has become a natural part of modern living. It plays while cooking, working, showering, having dinner with friends or simply in the background when you come home. Yet in many construction and renovation projects, audio is considered far too late, often only when the walls are already closed and the only remaining options are mobile speakers on furniture or plugged into sockets.

Good audio is by no means only a topic for audiophiles. It is not about analysing every detail of a recording or turning the living room into a studio. It is about integrating music and sound into everyday life in a clean, discreet and reliable way.

## Good sound is not a luxury problem

Many people say: “I am not an audiophile, normal sound is good enough for me.” That is understandable, but sound quality is often confused with high-end equipment.

Good sound does not necessarily mean expensive speakers, visible technology or excessive volume. Good sound means that music is pleasant to listen to, speech remains clear and sound is distributed evenly throughout the room. This is exactly what matters in everyday use.

A single speaker on a sideboard can play music, but it often fills a room unevenly. Close to the speaker it is too loud, further away it is too quiet. In open living areas, kitchens or dining rooms, this often leads to raising the volume even though the real problem is poor distribution.

Built-in speakers solve this problem more elegantly. They are placed where the sound is needed and disappear visually into the ceiling or wall. The result is a calmer, more pleasant listening experience, even at low volume.

## Multiroom audio is more than music in several rooms

A good multiroom system is not simply a collection of speakers. It is part of the room planning.

In the living area, music can play discreetly in the background. In the kitchen, it should remain clear despite cooking noises. In the bathroom, it can start automatically with the morning routine. The terrace requires a different solution than the bedroom. And in the TV area, the requirements are different again because speech, film sound and music come together.

This is exactly why multiroom audio should be planned early. Anyone who adds speakers, amplifiers, network and control afterwards will often have to accept compromises. If, on the other hand, audio is integrated into the electrical planning and building automation from the beginning, the result is technically clean, visually discreet and expandable in the long term.

## Why Sonos is often chosen

Sonos has made multiroom audio accessible to many people. The operation is simple, the app is well known, streaming services are easy to integrate and for individual rooms the system often works very well.

For rented apartments, smaller setups or flexible retrofits, Sonos can be a very sensible solution. There are also ways to combine Sonos with built-in speakers, for example with a Sonos Amp.

So Sonos is not wrong. On the contrary: for many applications, Sonos is a good product.

The more important question is: Is Sonos the best system solution for this specific project?

## Why Sonos is not always the right answer

In a detached house, a villa, a high-end renovation or a new build, the requirements change. It is no longer just about playing music somewhere. It is about architecture, operating logic, reliability, scalability and integration.

Sonos is primarily a strong consumer ecosystem. It is easy to use and works well within its own world. However, as soon as several zones, central equipment rooms, KNX, Loxone, Control4, RTI, TV audio, door communication, scene control or professional network planning are involved, the solution needs to be examined more carefully.

As the complexity of a project increases, topics such as centralised control, integration into building automation, a clean technical structure in the rack and long-term maintainability become increasingly important. In such cases, it is no longer enough to simply choose a product. What matters is a well thought-out overall concept that considers all requirements and also allows future extensions.

## The advantage of built-in speakers

Built-in speakers have one major advantage: they become part of the building, not part of the furniture.

They do not stand around, they do not need visible power cables and they do not take up space. Especially in high-quality living spaces, this is a decisive point. The technology should work, but it should not dominate the room.

In addition, in-ceiling or in-wall speakers can be matched to the room. A kitchen has different requirements than a bathroom. A dining area needs a different sound distribution than a home cinema area. Outdoor areas have their own requirements for moisture, temperature and installation.

Another advantage is longevity. High-quality speakers can easily be used for decades and age much more slowly than the associated electronics. Streaming technologies, apps and network components in particular develop rapidly and are regularly replaced by new standards. If the system is clearly divided into speakers, amplifiers and streaming components, individual elements can be replaced when needed without renewing the entire installation.

## Non-audiophiles benefit too

The biggest misconception is that only music lovers benefit from proper audio planning. In practice, the opposite is often true.

Audiophiles consciously sit down and listen to music. Most other people use audio in the background. That is exactly why it needs to work simply, pleasantly and naturally.

Nobody wants to think about which speaker needs to be switched on every time. Nobody wants visible cables, batteries to charge or mobile speakers to carry from room to room. And nobody wants to turn up a speaker on a sideboard during dinner just so that something can still be heard at the other end of the room.

A good multiroom system removes this friction.

## Planning before product

The most important recommendation is therefore: plan first, then choose the products.

A good concept answers the following questions in advance:

Which rooms should have audio?\
Is the focus on background music, TV sound, party mode or high-quality music playback?\
Which rooms need separate zones?\
Where should speakers be integrated invisibly?\
Where does a central amplifier solution make sense?\
How will the system be controlled?\
How will it be integrated into the network and building automation?\
What are the requirements for maintenance and expandability?

Only then should the decision be made whether Sonos, a professional multiroom system, a combination with KNX/Loxone/Control4/RTI or a hybrid solution is the best fit.

## Conclusion

Audio is not only a topic for audiophiles. It is a topic for everyone who values comfort, atmosphere and clean integration.

Sonos can be a very good system, but it is not automatically the best solution for every project. Especially in new builds, renovations and high-quality residential environments, it is worth considering audio early instead of solving it afterwards with mobile speakers.
