# Why early planning is so important

A modern smart home does not begin when the first switches are installed, lights are connected or apps are configured. The most important phase starts much earlier: during planning.

Especially in new builds, renovations or major refurbishments, the early project phase determines whether the technical infrastructure will later work reliably, flexibly and sustainably, or whether expensive compromises will be needed afterwards. Planning this topic too late often leads not only to additional costs, but also to limited possibilities, unnecessary complexity and solutions that are difficult to expand in the long term.

## More than an app

Many people initially think of an app that controls lighting, shading, music or heating. In practice, however, it is about much more than that.

What matters is the interaction between different trades: lighting, shading, heating, cooling, ventilation, access control, security, audio/video and network infrastructure. For these areas to work together reliably, a solid technical foundation is required. This does not happen spontaneously on site, but through forward-looking planning.

The technology should simplify everyday life, not make it more complicated. It should work in the background, respond logically and continue to function even when no smartphone is at hand.

## Early planning prevents expensive mistakes

One of the most common mistakes is addressing the topic only after the electrical planning has already been completed or the shell construction phase is well advanced. At that point, cable routes are defined, boxes are placed, distribution boards are dimensioned and interfaces can often no longer be implemented properly.

Typical consequences include:

- missing cables for switches, sensors or network connections
- undersized electrical distribution boards
- poorly positioned switches or control points
- missing conduits for future extensions
- unclear interfaces between electrician, HVAC, lighting design and AV
- retrofitted wireless solutions, even though a wired solution would have been possible
- systems that operate next to each other instead of with each other

Many of these problems can be avoided if the technical infrastructure is considered part of the overall planning from the beginning. Not as an add-on, but as a fixed component of the building.

## The right questions must be asked early

Good planning does not begin with the question of which product should be used. It begins with the question of how the building will be used.

How do residents move through the house? Which rooms are used and when? Where are scenes useful? Where is manual control important? Where should the system react automatically? Which functions should be implemented today and which options should remain open for the future?

Especially for lighting, shading, room climate and multimedia, this early coordination is crucial. If it is missed, solutions may work technically but feel unintuitive in everyday use.

An example: a switch at the entrance can be much more than just a light switch. When leaving the house, it can turn off all lights, move shading into a safe position, stop music, reduce standby consumption and set the house to away mode. For this to work, the individual systems must be planned correctly and connected cleanly from the start.

## Robust integration instead of DIY workarounds

Many solutions appear simple at first glance. Individual devices can be set up quickly, apps are user-friendly and automations can often be created by the user. For individual functions, this can work perfectly well.

It becomes problematic when many separate island solutions are expected to control an entire building.

DIY workarounds are often based on various apps, cloud services, wireless standards, temporary fixes and compromises. This may seem inexpensive and flexible in the short term, but often becomes confusing in the long run. As soon as a manufacturer changes its cloud service, a device is no longer supported or an update changes a function, troubleshooting begins.

A robust integration follows a different approach. It relies on clear structures, documented interfaces, reliable communication and systems designed for long-term operation. The goal is not to make everything look as “smart” as possible, but to ensure that the solution remains stable, understandable and maintainable.

## Wired where it makes sense

Wireless solutions have their place, especially for retrofits or individual additions. However, where cables can easily be planned in a new build or renovation, a wired solution is usually the more robust long-term choice.

Wired systems are less susceptible to interference, do not require batteries in central control elements and are easier to document. They create a solid foundation that can be expanded later.

This does not mean that every detail has to be implemented from the beginning. What matters much more is preparing the infrastructure correctly: enough space in the distribution board, sensible cable routing, network connections in the right places, conduits for future extensions and a clear system architecture.

## Mistakes that can be avoided

Many problems are not caused by bad products, but by a lack of coordination. Technical planning touches several specialist areas. When everyone only looks at their own trade, gaps quickly appear.

The electrician plans the basic installation. The lighting designer focuses on luminaires and moods. The heating planner concentrates on room control. The AV integrator plans music, TV and network infrastructure. Without overarching coordination, duplicate control points, missing interfaces or functions that can only be connected later with great effort quickly arise.

Typical mistakes include:

- lighting groups are not planned according to use and furniture layout
- shading is not combined with sun position, weather station or room climate
- heating control remains isolated and cannot react to presence or absence
- network infrastructure is dimensioned too tightly
- multimedia is only considered after interior finishing
- central control points are missing
- technical documentation is neglected

These mistakes can be avoided when it is clear early on which functions are desired and how the systems should work together.

## Long-term value instead of short-term comfort

Such an installation is not a short-term purchase like a single gadget. It becomes part of the building structure. That is why it should function reliably for many years, remain expandable and stay open to future requirements.

This is exactly why it is worth investing in clean planning and robust integration. Not every function has to be implemented immediately. But the foundation must be right.

A good solution grows with the needs of the residents. Today, the focus may be on lighting and shading. Later, energy management, access control, audio/video or security functions may be added. If the foundation has been planned correctly, such extensions can be integrated cleanly.

## The best technology is the one you do not constantly have to operate

A successful solution is not measured by how many apps are installed. It is measured by how much easier everyday routines become.

The lighting suits the situation. The shading reacts to sun, weather and time of day. The room temperature remains comfortable. Music and media can be operated intuitively. When leaving the house, one press of a button is enough. And if something needs to be adjusted, the system is logically structured and clearly documented.

This is exactly the difference between a collection of individual smart devices and a thoughtfully planned, professionally implemented installation.

## Conclusion

Early planning not only saves costs, but also prevents technical compromises. It ensures that lighting, shading, climate, network, security and multimedia work together in a meaningful way.

Those who plan early create the foundation for a reliable, durable and expandable system. Those who plan too late often have to live with retrofits, isolated solutions and unnecessary limitations.

Robust integration does not mean installing the most complicated technology possible. It means making the right decisions early, coordinating systems properly and creating a solution that works reliably in everyday life.
